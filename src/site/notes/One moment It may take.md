Sixteen years ago the myth of Plato's Atlantis found its way into the real world. This was the name of Alexander McQueen’s 2010 Spring/Summer womenswear collection and runway show. He used the story as a conceptual frame for imagining a future world shaped by flooding and environmental collapse. McQueen also connected that idea to Darwinian evolution. Garments were designed to give the appearance of reptiles and sea creatures, including snakes, stingrays, and jellyfish, while models were made to look hybrid or post-human, with fins, scales, shells, and other aquatic attributes. It was a proposition that humanity might adapt by evolving back toward aquatic life. The human body, pressed by rising seas, begins to assume new and unsettling forms. The collection was structured as a kind of visual evolution, moving from land and reptile references toward sea-creature forms and aquatic color palettes. One of the first major runway shows to be livestreamed online, it opens with a projected movie of a nude woman lying in sand. Soon snakes begin to appear, and she is covered by them. With a slightly erotic undertone, you almost get the sense that she is enraptured, making love to the reptiles. Next a psychedelic animation covers the screen, making it seem as though you had woken up in a pit of them yourself. All of a sudden, all sorts of truly avant garde(n) women begin to appear. Incredibly serious demeanor paired with quite lavish clothing designs, and otherworldly makeup/hair, it is like another species rose from the waters - to the runway. They strut as though just coming back into the house after that thanksgiving "walk" with the cousins, taken to help dinner digest. Really a beautiful show, I found myself in awe of the shoes they were wearing, what became the most remembered relics of the occasion. The "Armadillo." Only 24 exist across the planet today. 21 were originally designed for the show, and then 3 more were recreated for a charity event which took place in 2015. They were so tall that some models refused to walk in them. Lady Gaga wore the pair in her Bad Romance video, and it was at this Plato's Atlantis runway show that the song was first premiered. It turns out that this was a turbulent time in Alexanders life. The original plan was to have a box on stage, and then, at the end, walk down the runway himself, open it up, and end the show with a "bang." He couldn't go through with it. There was a mixture of cocaine, sleeping pills, and also a smaller amount of tranquillizers found in his system, but had hung himself. Palenque was a Mayan city in what is now Chiapas, Mexico, flourishing roughly between 600 and 750 CE. Its original Maya name was Lakam’ha which means ”Place of many Waters”. The monuments housed here were early and innovative compared with other major centers, such as Tikal, and because so many of its texts survived, Palenque became a key for understanding Maya history and religion. It is one of the most studied sites which revealed a trove of information about the way these people worked, a sorta Rosetta stone, with many discoveries concerning the decipherment of glyphic texts based on its carved inscriptions. The royal lineage that lived here has now been documented from the 5th through to the 8th centuries, with births, accessions to the throne, deaths, victories and defeats - all imprinted in stone throughout the site. Archaeological evidence, in the form of pottery fragments, shows that people were living in Palenque as early as the Middle Preclassic period (around 600–300 BCE). Over time the site grew, and by the Early Classic period (250–600 CE) large public buildings were being constructed, marking the start of the cities written history with its first known ruler, K’uk’ Bahlam(Quetzal Jaguar), who took the throne on March 10, 431 CE. Inscriptions denoting the first three early Kings mention them as ruling from an unknown site named Tok Tahn, before relocating to Lakam’ha/Palenque. The corpus of glyphic inscriptions is extensive, and has helped piece together political/social relations with other sites, and insight into Maya rituals and world views. Concepts of kingship, time, and the journey between underworld and heavens are all major themes. At the heart of this location is the _Temple of the Inscriptions_, a nine‑tiered pyramid whose long hieroglyphic panels and hidden tomb turned Palenque from a place of pebbled ruins, into a residency of rhapsodical renovation, with a single discovery....

Ruz Lhuillier was the man who uncovered the tomb of Pacal. It all began in March, walking across the Temple of the Inscriptions during his first official visit to Palenque in 1947. He was originally sent out here to investigate complaints filed by Postmaster Joaquín Hernández Palacios, who alleged that site guardian Agustin Álvarez was neglecting the ruins. Just to get out there was a journey, the area being extremely isolated, and from Mérida, it took four days by bus, train, chiclero plane, and horseback. When he arrived, Ruz documented eight primary structures, noted water seepage, a little bit of black slime on architecture, weather damage, and uncleared excavation debris from previous work. The report said there was garbage all over the place, but there wasn't - just regular rubble from fallen buildings. As-well, it was said that the camp house, the building that houses the archaeologists during excavation season, was amess. Ruz found it to be clean and in good condition, except for an opening in the roof from natural causes. He concluded that the site's deterioration was due to a wet climate and dense jungle growth, not negligence - especially since no excavations had occurred in two years. After interviewing the Postmaster, guardians, and townspeople, Ruz concluded the complaint was driven by jealousy, bad faith, and a financial dispute. 

In October 1946, Postmaster Hernández visited the ruins but filed no report at that time. He saw whatever he saw, but sat on it for five full months before filing anything. Later down the line, around February 22nd, 1947, is when he sends the first negative report, claiming that the site had been abandoned by its guardian. Shortly after that period, 2-22-47, is when his red nosed employee Gonzales Rodolfo went and borrowed 200 pesos from this site guardian, telling him that money was needed for the next inspection hearing. The particularly damning part, exposing a little bit of potential calculation, is the fact that he was given a receipt. Gonzales didn't just take the money and disappear, well, he actually did disappear on a month long vacation, but first he gave Álvarez a written receipt, which should have been proof of their exchange. This receipt, however, was deliberately worded to make Álvarez look like the debtor, who owed 200 pesos, not the one with money in reserves. So when Álvarez went to Hernandez and tries to push back, the man claimed to know nothing about the situation, and in rebuttal to this folly, all Alvarez had was a piece of paper in his hand that legally worked against him, rather than for him. On March 11th, 1947 - Hernández reaffirmed his complaint and pushed for Álvarez to be replaced, essentially filing a second notice. March 15th, 1947, that is when Ruz arrived at the site and began his investigation.

Due to the fact that when he arrived everything was all good, Ruz suspected a deliberate scheme to get Álvarez fired, and noted this in his writings :

"My personal impression – resulting from my discussions with the guardians and with the aforementioned postmaster - is that there is a direct relationship between the matter of money taken from Agustin Álvarez and the insistence that he be dismissed as soon as possible. I cannot specify who is the instigator of this conflict and what is the end sought, but it is conceivable that harm is wanted and it is in bad faith. The evidence of such bad faith are mostly false reports about the work of the guardian, how the money was taken and the fact that the "receipt" that was given him for the 200 pesos is worded in such a way that it appears that Agustín Álvarez is the debtor and therefore he cannot legally claim what is owed"

Being an astute investigator, Ruz began to ask questions to the people of the town in regard to the character of all parties involved. He found that there was a dislike and envy amongst the local populace towards the site guardian and his stepson. They were getting paid 300 pesos a month, and were equipped with poultry, pigs, and even horses to ride. Ruz concluded his report by going over the fact that the guardian, Agustin, was a very hardworking, serious, and honest man. He fulfilled his obligations for the archaeological zone, and Ruz was confident that he could continue to do his job into the foreseeable future. 

With the petty administrative dispute settled and the guardian's reputation secured, Ruz submitted his findings. But his initial 1947 inspection had instigated the inspiration of something else entirely internally. While surveying the ruins of recklessness only to titillatingly disprove the postmaster's crossed claims of negligence, Ruz recognized the immense, untapped archaeological potential of Palenque. Returning to his superiors, he successfully petitioned the INAH to authorize and fund a formal, long-term excavation. In the spring of 1948, Ruz returned to the jungle joyful. Instead of being a tedious errand boy, now he was the official director of his own archaeological project. What had begun as investigations into a fabricated 200-peso debt, was about to provocatively pivot into one of the most significant moments in Mesoamerican history.

In return to the precious land where he would come to make memories with Pals in which (en que) he would remember forever, Ruz found himself drawn, as if by a spell, exactly to the place where his attention needed to be. He then observed exactly what needed to be observed. Unlike the typical stucco floors seen in many Maya temples, the sanctuary floor of the Temple of the Inscriptions was formed of large, tightly fitted flagstones. Ruz saw that one prominent central slab had several holes cut through it, and that these had been filled with carefully fitted stone plugs. These were anomalous features in what appeared to be a finished floor. Interpreting theme as concealed lifting-holes used by the ancient builders to maneuver the heavy stone, he had his team remove the slab. Beneath it, workers encountered the top of a narrow, rubble-packed shaft that proved to be the uppermost portion of a descending internal staircase. 

The passage leading downward into the pyramid was completely filled to the brim, and would take them years to eventually open fully. Workers began clearing downward and had exposed 23 steps by the end of the 1948 summer season. Continued clearing in subsequent seasons finally brought the team, in June 1952, to the bottom of a 59 step stairway and to the final, sealed stone doorway that blocked access to the chamber beyond. Just listen to the way he describes what was found through that portal, it sounds like a love poem, as though he were remarking on a moment in which his heart had fluttered. The Maya people played ball. In-fact, they may have been the first to ever play ball, or any sport, with this sport of theirs dated to go back 3,000+ years. 

More than 1,500 ballcourts have been discovered in Mesoamerica altogether. They were often placed in the ceremonial center of ancient sites, close to important temples and funerary shrines, with some sites have many ballcourts. Although they were primarily associated with the ballgame, archaeological excavation indicates that they were used for other purposes as well, like feasting, ritual performances, and other types of sports, including boxing. In the Aztec region, it was common for spectators to gamble on the game. Evidence also suggests the ballgame was a noisy affair, attracting a crowd that would become passionate about the performances at hand. Most of the courts were shaped similar to the letter capital I. The ball had to constantly stay in motion, and players were not allowed to use their hands or feet to throw or kick it. Having to use their bodies, particularly the hip, it was a physically taxing event - which in some cases facilitated the wearing of protective gear. The balls that they used were made out of rubber, something they learned to create from native rubber-producing plants long before other places around the world. It could range from the size of a softball up to a soccer ball, and sometimes weighed up to 20 pounds. Portable court markers known as _hacha_, the Spanish word for “axes”, were placed around the arena as a sort of decoration, or as ritual effigies during important games. They were thin, flat stones that the Maya and other Mesoamerican peoples carved into shapes like human heads, skulls, or animals. In some cases, it seems players would attach these stones to their yoke, which was a belt‑like piece worn around the hips. Artistic depictions of the game lead us to think that these stones representatives may have been solidified trophies of a much lighter and more perishable material, used as part of their protective gear.
